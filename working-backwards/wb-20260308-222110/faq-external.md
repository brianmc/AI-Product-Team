## Q1. The press release says my reservation is "guaranteed." What actually happens if I arrive and the venue says they have no record of my booking — or simply has no table?

**Answer:** The press release uses the word "guaranteed" without defining what backstop exists when a venue fails to honor it. Two scenarios need an honest answer:

- **Booking failure before arrival:** If the venue cannot honor the reservation (unexpected closure, private event, capacity issue), there is no stated protocol for re-routing the cardholder to an alternative venue that evening, compensating them, or even notifying them proactively.

## Q2. These venues are described as places that "don't advertise" and "don't take OpenTable reservations." Why would they give a fintech card benefit access they don't give to anyone else — and how does that change the experience for me?

**Answer:** This is the central tension in the product. Exclusive venues derive part of their value from scarcity. If the bar is "the kind of place a local regular would know," the proposition to that venue to onboard with High Flyer is unclear: what do they gain, and how much volume are they committing to?

Two risks follow for cardholders:

1. **Watered-down access.** If venues are not fully bought in, they may provide compliant-but-reluctant service: a real table, but not the experience the PR describes. The bar that treats High Flyer bookings as obligation fulfillment rather than welcomed guests is not the same bar the customer quote describes.
2. **Shrinking inventory.** If venues find that High Flyer bookings displace regulars or damage the exclusivity that defines them, they may quietly reduce their allocated slots or exit the program. A cardholder who had a great experience in Chicago in April may find the same venue unavailable in October.

The press release does not explain the venue value proposition or the contractual commitment venues make. `[OPEN — owner: Partnerships]`

## Q9. The booking flow clearly knows where I go, when I go, and which venues I visit. What does Xfinite do with that data — and does any of it get shared with the venues I book through High Flyer?

**Answer:** Every High Flyer booking generates a behavioral record: which city, which venue, which date, and how often. Across a year of use, that data constitutes a detailed profile of a cardholder's travel patterns, social habits, and discretionary spend behavior. This audience — frequent travelers, high income — is precisely the segment that advertisers, data brokers, and the venues themselves would value access to.

The press release says nothing about any of the following:

- **Internal use:** Does Xfinite use High Flyer booking data to inform credit decisions, cross-sell other card products, or target marketing? Is booking history kept separate from the cardholder's financial profile, or does it merge into it?
- **Venue visibility:** What booking data does a venue receive? A name and party size for the reservation is operationally necessary. But does the venue also receive booking history, cardholder tier, or spend information? Can venues see how often a cardholder has visited or whether they are a "high-value" customer?
- **Third-party sharing:** Is any anonymized or aggregated High Flyer data sold or licensed to third parties — hospitality analytics firms, for example?
- **Retention and deletion:** How long is booking history retained? Can a cardholder request deletion of their High Flyer history independent of their main Xfinite account?

None of these questions can be answered from the press release. All four are unresolved and require explicit policy decisions before the product can be accurately described to prospective cardholders.
